Week 3: Empathize & Define – Self-Cleaning Dog Brush

Date: (11/11/2025)

Quick Summary

  • Skills Learned: Empathy mapping, user observation, defining user needs
  • Digital Tools and Software Used: pinterst , photoshop
  • Topics: Empathize & Define stages of the Design Thinking process
  • Key Concepts: User emotions, pain points, and design opportunity identification
  • Challenges Faced: Turning user frustration into a clear problem statement

Introduction

This week focused on the first two phases of the Design Thinking process – Empathize and Define. The selected object was a dog grooming brush that often clogs with hair after use. The goal was to understand the user's frustrations and define a clear design problem that could inspire a new self-cleaning dog brush concept.

Empathize & Define Process

Step 1 – Pick an Annoying Object

Object: Dog grooming brush
Frustration: After brushing their pet, users struggle to remove the clumps of hair trapped between the bristles. The process is slow, unpleasant, and discourages regular grooming.

Step 2 – Empathize with Users

CategoryWhat the User...Examples
Says“Ugh, it’s full of hair again!” / “It’s so hard to clean this brush.”
Thinks“There must be an easier way to remove all this hair.” / “I wish it could clean itself.”
DoesPulls hair out with fingers, hits the brush against surfaces hence damage brush bristles, or stops halfway through cleaning.
FeelsFrustrated, annoyed, and sometimes grossed out by the buildup of dog hair.

Insight: The cleaning stage of grooming is consistently ignored by current brush designs. Every grooming session ends with frustration, showing a clear opportunity for a self-cleaning or easy-release brush design.

Step 3 – Define the Problem

Dog owners need a way to easily remove trapped hair from their brushes because current designs clog quickly and are time-consuming and unpleasant to clean.

Step 4 – Reflection

Empathy revealed that even a small, everyday annoyance—like cleaning pet hair from a brush—can inspire meaningful design innovation. By defining the problem clearly, future concepts can aim to create brushes that self-release hair or simplify cleaning with minimal user effort.

Reflection

This exercise emphasized how understanding users deeply can transform ordinary frustrations into design opportunities. I learned to observe user behavior beyond the product’s primary function and identify secondary pain points that strongly influence satisfaction. The clogging dog brush problem demonstrated the value of empathy-driven design in shaping practical, human-centered solutions.
