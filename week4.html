Introduction

3D modeling involves the creation of three-dimensional objects utilizing specialized computer programs. It entails creating digital objects that can be viewed, rotated, and handled within a virtual environment. 3D models find applications in engineering, product design, animation, gaming, architecture, manufacturing, and numerous other areas. 3D modeling enables designers and engineers to convert concepts into digital prototypes that can be examined, improved, and visualized prior to actual production. The procedure involves manipulating shapes, meshes, surfaces, and volumes to create precise models that depict actual or conceptual objects. Contemporary 3D design software like SolidWorks, Blender, Fusion 360, and AutoCAD offers robust functionalities for crafting accurate mechanical parts or intricate artistic creations. Regardless of the objective being realistic rendering, animation, 3D printing, or engineering design, 3D modeling serves as the basis of digital creation

Reflection

Gaining skills in 3D modeling with Blender and SolidWorks has been a fulfilling and enlightening journey. Initially, I considered the interfaces difficult since every software features its unique workflow, shortcuts, and design approach. Blender seemed more creative and adaptable, whereas SolidWorks was stringent, accurate, and geared towards engineering. Alternating between the two allowed me to grasp the distinction between creative modeling and parametric modeling
